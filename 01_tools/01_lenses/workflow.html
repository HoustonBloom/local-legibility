Workflow

How the publishing pipeline actually works.

Content goes in as tagged markdown files. Cuts shape it through lenses. Frames come out as self-contained HTML. Windows bundle frames into tabbed packages.

Core concepts

Five pieces, one pipeline.

Input

Content

Your markdown files. Tagged with a content-tag in their visibility array to mark them as source material for a specific cut. No tag, no inclusion.

Config

Cut

A config file that defines what goes in (content-tag), who it's for (audience), how it sounds (voice/narrative), and which lens renders it.

Layout

Frame

The rendered output. One cut produces one frame: a self-contained HTML file shaped by the lens, styled by the design system. Open it in any browser.

Bundle

Window

Multiple frames bundled into a single HTML file with tabbed navigation. One URL, many views. This file you are reading is a window.

Mood

Atmosphere

An optional ambient layer beneath the lens content. Particle field, gradient wash, grain texture. Sits at z-index 0; the lens content reads on top of it. Set in the cut config; lenses honor it without changing structure.

Tuning for an audience

Same content, different delivery.

When a cut targets a specific person, the rendering tunes to them. The system keeps a small profile per contact in 01_tools/05_contacts/<Name>.md. When that name appears in the cut config's audience: field, their preferences get layered on top of the lens defaults. Same project, same lens, two different audiences, two correctly-shaped frames.

Priority 01 · highest Cut config body

What this specific cut says.

Anything written under Voice, Structure, Emphasis, De-emphasis, or Visual Direction in the cut config wins. Per-cut overrides live here.

↓ falls through to ↓
Priority 02 · middle Contact overlay

What this person prefers.

If the cut config is silent on a section, the agent reads the contact's Rendering Preferences section and applies it. Activated by setting rendering-profile: true in the contact's frontmatter.

↓ falls through to ↓
Priority 03 · lowest Lens defaults

What the lens spec ships with.

If neither the cut config nor the contact provides a section, the lens spec's defaults render. Every cut has a working baseline even with no overrides.

Sections resolve independently. A cut config can override Voice while leaving Emphasis to the contact overlay and Visual Direction to the lens defaults. Style-source tokens (color, typography) are baked in last and apply regardless of which layer supplied the voice direction.

What gets overlaid

Four sections inside Rendering Preferences.

Visual Language

Palette, typography, reference-implementation file, motifs they respond to.

Information Architecture

How they scan vs. read. Density. Section ordering. Stats cards, file lists, collapsibles.

What to Emphasize

What to lead with. The kind of evidence they actually weigh.

What to De-emphasize

What to compact, hide, or move below the fold.

When to fill this in

Copy 01_tools/05_contacts/_template.md to <Full Name>.md when you start working with someone. Fill in Who They Are, Shared Work, and basic notes on How to Talk to Them immediately.

Leave Rendering Preferences blank at first. Fill it in only after the pattern is clear. Set rendering-profile: true in frontmatter once it's populated, so the cut-packet skill knows the section is load-bearing. Inventing a person's preferences from a single interaction tends to cost trust.

Working with the packet

What to ask your AI.

The packet is built for human + AI. You don't run scripts or copy templates by hand. You tell your AI what you want, and it handles the implementation against the specs in 01_tools/. The packet ships six skills your AI uses to do the work: three slash commands for session hygiene, plus cut-packet, yaml-audit, and figma-mcp.

Session rituals · run these as you go
/scan

At session start.

Drop anything into 00_inbox/ as you work (voice memos, screenshots, half-formed ideas). /scan walks the inbox, surfaces pending flags, recent activity, and last session's handoff.

/changelog

As you ship.

When you finish something meaningful, ask your AI to log it. One line, project-tagged, written to Daily/Changelog.md. Builds an arc you can scan over weeks.

/complete

At session end.

Hand the next session a baton. One or two sentences naming what's next. The next /scan reads it back.

Make a frame · the workhorse

The cut-packet prompt.

Cut me a frame of [topic or content tag] using the [lens] lens for [audience].

Your AI reads the lens spec, finds the matching content in your folder, applies the contact overlay if you named a specific person, and writes a self-contained HTML frame to 03_cuts/.

Pitch competitions: the pitch lens has its own routing flow. Say "I'm prepping a pitch for [audience]" and your AI asks four questions (capital type, time budget, audience, stage) before generating an 11-slide deck.

Other useful prompts

Add a contact

Add a contact profile for [name].

Your AI copies 01_tools/05_contacts/_template.md, fills the basics, leaves Rendering Preferences blank until you know the pattern.

Tune a contact's rendering preferences

Update [name]'s profile. They respond well to [pattern].

Your AI fills the Rendering Preferences section and flips rendering-profile: true. Next cut to that audience picks it up automatically.

Audit your folder's frontmatter

Run yaml-audit on my folder.

Your AI walks the vault, reports drift (missing required fields, inconsistent casing, typo'd field names), optionally fixes in place. Run before a packet rebuild.

Add a new lens

I want a new lens for [use case]. Walk me through it.

Your AI copies the lens template, drafts the spec with you, builds an example, validates against the conventions in 01_tools/01_lenses/ADDING-A-LENS.md. Same pattern for atmospheres.

Log a system change

Log this to the activity log: [what changed and why].

Your AI writes a dated entry into 02_activity/ with the appropriate principle tags. See the next section for what the log captures.

If you don't know the right prompt, describe what you want in plain language. The AI reads the specs and asks for what it needs. The packet bends to you, not the other way around.
Activity log

A running journal of how your system changes.

Logs the system itself, not the work inside it. When a new lens gets added, a folder restructures, a contact profile gets promoted from blank-rendering-prefs to populated, that's logged in 02_activity/. Different from a project changelog, which tracks work inside projects. This tracks the shape of the system.

You don't write entries by hand. Tell your AI what changed and why; it writes the entry. Over time the log becomes onboarding context: a new AI instance opens your folder, reads recent activity, and understands why your setup looks the way it does today.

Substrate, not lens.

The log is data. Downstream lenses can read it to produce views: principle time-series, quarter reports, constellation activity. The log itself stays readable as markdown.

Fresh-AI context.

When a new agent instance opens your folder, it reads recent activity entries to understand why the system looks the way it does today. The log is onboarding context made durable.

Tagged against principles.

Each entry can carry principles: [exit, sufficiency] in its frontmatter. Over time you can ask "everything I've done in service of Provide an Exit this quarter," and the log answers.

Deprecate, never delete.

When an entry becomes obsolete, your AI marks it status: deprecated and adds a superseded-by: pointer. History stays traceable. Same rule applies to lenses, atmospheres, contacts.
